Why do we care about ODEs?

First-order linear ordinary differential equations (ODEs) are a type of differential equation that have many simple yet powerful applications. Here are a few examples of applications of first-order linear ODEs:

  1. Chemical reactions: First-order linear ODEs are often used to model the rate of change of the concentration of a chemical reactant as a function of time. The rate of change is typically modeled by the derivative of the concentration with respect to time.

  2. Radioactive decay: The rate of decay of a radioactive substance can be modeled by a first-order linear ODE. The solution to this ODE gives the amount of the substance remaining as a function of time.

  3. Electrical circuits: First-order linear ODEs can be used to model the voltage or current in an electric circuit as a function of time. This can be used to predict how a circuit will behave under different conditions and to design new circuits.

  4. Economics: Applications to economics are also used to model the behavior of consumer demand, price dynamics, market growth or decline etc. which is also based on first order linear differential equations.

  5. Mechanical systems: Simple mechanical systems such as a mass-spring system or a system consisting of a sliding block on an inclined plane can be modeled using first-order linear ODEs. These equations are used to predict the position, velocity, and acceleration of the object over time.

  6. Biological systems: First-order linear ODEs can be used to model population growth, which describes how the population of a certain species changes over time depending on the birth rate and death rate.

These are just a few examples of the many simple applications of first-order linear ODEs, but it's important to note that this type of differential equation can be found in many fields and can be used to model a wide range of phenomena.  Our first unit will be completely centered around these equations.

Pro Tips

One of the big differences is noticing the solutions to our problems are functions, not values. So we are often after a family of functions that behaves in a certain way. This is more general (and powerful) than solving a "static" equation.

Get Your Hands Dirty

  1. Is the function \(y = 2e^{-x}\) a solution to the differential equation \(dy/dx + 2y = 0\)?
    Answer: Yes
  2. Verify that \(y = x^2\) is a solution to the differential equation \(y' = 2x\).
    Answer: Yes
  3. Check if \(y = \cos(x)\) is a solution to the differential equation \(y'' + y = 0\)
    Ans: Yes
  4. Can you verify if \( y = xe^x \) is a solution to the differential equation \(y' + xy = x^2\) ?
    Ans: No.
