Some Notes on Limits From Lecture

The limits at the end can be challenging to wrap our heads around.

The issue is that multiple things could happen. The easiest two cases is if

(1) z=f(x,y) is continuous, so we can just evaluate it at the point, and

(2) if z=f(x,y) has a discontinuity and we can show the limit does not exist by proving the limit from y=f(x) doesn't equal the limit as we approach from y=g(x). OR We can show it does exist by using a tool like L'Hopitals rule, the squeeze theorem, or showing the discontinuity is removable by some algebraic manipulation.

But it is easy to find examples where (1) and (2) both don't prove if it exists or does not.

The next question is obviously "how do we show it in general?". And that takes us to the formal definition of limits, which I do not emphasize in this course. But if you are heading into more pure mathematics, this is something you should really understand. For the purposes of this course, the above two cases will suffice for our working understanding of how limits work in R3 & Rn.
